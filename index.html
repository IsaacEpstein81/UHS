Overview

AI training and live support for admissions teams

A practical starting point for UHS: facility-specific AI role plays for training, plus a real-time admissions co-pilot grounded in approved program and facility resources.

Two Practical Capabilities

What OpenRecovery can produce first for UHS.

Capability 01

Facility-specific AI role plays

Voice or text mock calls tailored by facility, level of care, insurance questions, objections, and scripting priorities.

Why it matters for UHS

Helps staff learn five to seven locations with different rules, offerings, and admissions considerations more efficiently.

Capability 02

Real-time admissions co-pilot

An AI assistant grounded in approved UHS program and facility information to support agents during live conversations and reinforce training afterward.

Why it matters for UHS

Gives agents faster access to the answers they need, reducing interruptions, callbacks, and missed placement opportunities.

Examples

How the two capabilities show up in practice.

Manual onboarding to practice before live calls

Five new staff members start. A senior team member hands over a training manual and then spends time fielding repeated questions while new hires learn on live calls. With OpenRecovery, staff can practice facility-specific scenarios with AI personas before speaking with real callers, then keep using the co-pilot to study and reinforce what they learned.

Missed opportunity to same-day action

A caller asks about a specific program or admission fit. Instead of placing the caller on hold or promising a callback, the agent uses the co-pilot in real time, gets the approved answer during the conversation, and is better positioned to move the caller toward placement that day.

Realistic Training

Train for the conversations people actually have.

Behavioral health admissions teams do not only get calm, polite, textbook callers. OpenRecovery can simulate emotionally charged interactions so staff can practice de-escalation, empathy, and factual accuracy under real pressure.

Why this matters

  • Practice staying regulated while the caller is not
  • Coach empathy and escalation judgment in messy real-world moments
  • Prepare staff for objections, blame, fear, and urgency before live calls

Different from a standard LLM demo

The point is not to showcase a polite chatbot. It is to create realistic training pressure so agents can learn how to respond well when the other person is upset, confused, or volatile.
